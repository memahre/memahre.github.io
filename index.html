About

I am a Computational Social Science M.S. student at the University of California, San Diego with a background in psychology and communication. My work sits at the intersection of machine learning and the behavioral and cognitive sciences, where I use statistical and computational methods to study how AI systems behave, where that behavior diverges from human expectations, and whether our evaluation frameworks are actually measuring what we intend them to measure.

My graduate research focuses on multimodal foundation model evaluation, including cross-modal representation analysis, synthetic data pipeline construction, and behavioral testing of vision and audio models. I am particularly interested in evaluation validity to ask not just whether a metric correlates with performance, but whether it captures the right thing under the right conditions.

More broadly, I am interested in the gap between how models perform on benchmarks and how they actually behave when they meet real users and real decisions, and am drawn to understanding that gap through more valid evaluation frameworks, more careful attention to what human feedback encodes, and more honest measurement of what models can and cannot do.

Multimodal Foundation Model Evaluation
Building evaluation pipelines to assess semantic alignment between audio and vision foundation models and examine where cross-modal representations diverge.

Description

While much multimodal research focuses on language–vision or language-audio alignment, direct comparisons between auditory and visual models remain limited. This project builds an end-to-end multimodal evaluation pipeline to assess semantic alignment between audio and vision foundation models using the AudioCaps corpus. The core research question is whether models trained independently on audio and visual data develop similar internal representations of semantic concepts, and what cross-modal divergence reveals about how each model encodes meaning.

Data

Constructed a controlled vision-language evaluation set using a two-stage synthetic data curation pipeline. Audio captions from AudioCaps were expanded into richer visual descriptions using Phi-3, a large language model, and paired images were generated with Stable Diffusion XL. This approach enables direct semantic comparison across modalities with consistent content grounding.

Method

Extract audio embeddings using BEATs and image embeddings using DINOv2. Apply layer-wise Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA) and linear predictivity metrics to quantify cross-modal alignment across model layers and surface representation drift across architectures.

Strategic Communication Health Campaign on a College Campus
Evaluating the impact of a campus health intervention using behavior change theory and statistical analysis.

Description

This project applied behavioral science principles and behavior change theory to understand how people process health information, form intentions, and build confidence to act in support of others. The intervention examined how strategic communication design, grounded in psychological frameworks for attitude and behavior change, could shift both knowledge and self-efficacy among undergraduate students around campus safety and peer support.

Data

Pre- and post-intervention survey responses collected from approximately 400 undergraduate students before and after an educational presentation to measure knowledge of campus health resources and confidence in responding to peer support scenarios. Prior American College Health Association (ACHA) data were used to identify major campus health challenges and inform the target population for the intervention.

Method

Designed and implemented a campus health communication campaign consisting of informational materials and short presentations delivered to student organizations. Campaign effectiveness was evaluated using pre- and post-intervention surveys, and statistical analysis compared changes in knowledge and confidence measures. Independent t-tests and descriptive statistics were used to assess whether the intervention improved outcomes.

Outcome

The campaign produced statistically significant improvements in both resource knowledge and confidence. Average knowledge scores increased, and students reported greater confidence in responding to peer support situations after the intervention. The findings provided actionable insights for improving future student wellness programming.

Stress-Testing Bias Mitigation in Text-to-Image Models

Question

Do post-hoc bias mitigation methods for text-to-image models generalize across diffusion architectures, and do their effects remain consistent across prompt domains including stereotyped occupations, authority roles, and historically grounded roles?

Data

Custom prompt dataset of 16 roles across four categories: female-stereotyped occupations, male-stereotyped occupations, authority and leadership roles, and historically grounded roles. Categories were grounded in prior NLP and generative model bias research. Images were generated individually using Stable Diffusion XL and evaluated using per-image demographic classification with DeepFace.

Method

Applied FairImagen, a post-hoc bias mitigation framework introduced at NeurIPS 2025, to Stable Diffusion XL to extend the original evaluation from SD3 to a different dual-encoder architecture. Measured gender fairness, age fairness, and prompt-image alignment (CLIPScore) across baseline and FairImagen conditions. Ran a 2×2 hyperparameter sensitivity analysis across hidden dimension and empirical noise settings.

Result

FairImagen's gender fairness improvements did not generalize consistently to SDXL. Only female-stereotyped roles showed improvement (0.38 to 0.62), while authority and male-stereotyped roles showed no change. An unexpected finding was that age fairness improved across all four categories, including those where gender fairness was unchanged, suggesting FairPCA projection affects demographic attributes beyond its intended targets. Prompt-image alignment remained stable, indicating semantic fidelity was preserved. Results suggest FairImagen is architecture-dependent and its effects may be broader than intended.

Evaluating Moral Framing Bias in Large Language Models

Question

Do large language models exhibit the same omission bias observed in human moral psychology, where harmful actions (commissions) are judged as more wrong than equally harmful failures to act (omissions)?

Data

Constructed 14 commission–omission minimal pairs based on scenarios from moral psychology experiments. Each scenario was evaluated across four stimulus conditions using the target words “wrong” and “acceptable,” with and without explicit intent framing.

Method

Evaluated four autoregressive language models (GPT-2, GPT-2-Medium, GPT-2-Large, and GPT-Neo-125M) using next-token prediction to compute the conditional probability of moral evaluation words. Surprisal scores were used as a measure of implicit moral expectation, comparing how expected “wrong” or “acceptable” was following commission versus omission scenarios.

Result

Across models and conditions, LLMs did not reproduce the human omission bias. Instead, moral condemnation (“wrong”) was generally more expected following omission scenarios than commission scenarios, indicating that model predictions reflect statistical patterns in language rather than human-like moral reasoning.

Investigating Implicit Bias in Large Language Models

Question

How do model type and social category interact to predict implicit bias in large language models?

Data

Replication/extension analysis using Bai et al. (2024) dataset measuring implicit bias in large language models through a prompt-based adaptation of the Implicit Association Test (IAT) based on human psychology. Analyzed a subset of 2,400 observations (300 responses per model) from 8 models across 4 domains (gender, health, race, religion) and 21 stereotype categories.

Method

Fit linear regression models in R to estimate main effects of model and category, and model × category interaction effects. Tested whether prompt variation affected bias estimates and evaluated heteroscedasticity, reporting heteroscedasticity-robust standard errors.

Result

Found that implicit bias is highly context-dependent: bias levels vary across specific model–category combinations rather than being uniform across models or domains. The interaction model explained substantially more variance than model-only baselines, and prompt variation did not significantly change the bias patterns beyond the model–category interaction.
