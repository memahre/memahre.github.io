About

I am a Computational Social Science M.S. student at the University of California, San Diego with a background in psychology and communication. My work sits at the intersection of machine learning and the behavioral and cognitive sciences, where I use statistical and computational methods to study how models represent information and how their behavior can be measured and evaluated.

My research focuses on the empirical evaluation of modern machine learning models, such as using representation learning, multimodal models, and large-scale datasets to investigate how models encode semantic information and how representations align across modalities.

More broadly, I’m interested in human-centered approaches to machine learning and how careful evaluation, combined with insights from behavioral and cognitive science, can improve our understanding of model behavior and help support the development of more reliable and responsible AI systems.

Multimodal Model Evaluation and Interpretability
Study evaluating how audio and vision foundation models represent semantic information across modalities.

Description

While substantial research has examined language–vision and language–image model alignment, far less work has directly compared models trained on auditory and visual modalities. This project investigates how vision and audio foundation models represent semantic information and whether their internal representations align across modalities.

Data

Constructed a controlled multimodal dataset using audio clips and captions from the AudioCaps dataset. Corresponding images were generated with Stable Diffusion to ensure consistent semantic content across audio and visual inputs, enabling direct comparison of representations across modalities.

Method

Extract embeddings from audio and image inputs using pretrained foundation models (BEATs for audio and DINOv2 for vision). Apply representation similarity metrics, including Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) and Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA), to analyze cross-modal embedding geometry and measure representational alignment across layers and model architectures.

Strategic Communication Health Campaign on a College Campus
Study applying behavior change theory and statistical analysis to evaluate the impact of a real-world health intervention.

Description

This project examined how strategic communication and behavioral change theory could be used to improve health knowledge and engagement among undergraduate students at Santa Clara University.

Data

Pre- and post-intervention survey data collected from approximately 400 undergraduate students during a campus-wide strategic health campaign. The project also used prior American College Health Association (ACHA) data to identify major campus health challenges and define the target audience for the intervention.

Method

Designed and implemented a campus-wide health communication campaign informed by behavioral change theory. Developed educational resources, presented health information to students, and created a pathway for the Wellness Center to connect with the student body. Campaign impact was evaluated using pre- and post-test survey measures and statistical analysis conducted in SPSS.

Outcome

Statistical analysis showed a significant positive increase in health knowledge among participating students. The work provided actionable insights for future student wellness programming.

Evaluating Moral Framing Bias in Large Language Models

Question

Do large language models exhibit the same omission bias observed in human moral psychology, where harmful actions (commissions) are judged as more wrong than equally harmful failures to act (omissions)?

Data

Constructed 14 commission–omission minimal pairs based on scenarios from moral psychology experiments. Each scenario was evaluated across four stimulus conditions using the target words “wrong” and “acceptable,” with and without explicit intent framing.

Method

Evaluated four autoregressive language models (GPT-2, GPT-2-Medium, GPT-2-Large, and GPT-Neo-125M) using next-token prediction to compute the conditional probability of moral evaluation words. Surprisal scores were used as a measure of implicit moral expectation, comparing how expected “wrong” or “acceptable” was following commission versus omission scenarios.

Result

Across models and conditions, LLMs did not reproduce the human omission bias. Instead, moral condemnation (“wrong”) was generally more expected following omission scenarios than commission scenarios, indicating that model predictions reflect statistical patterns in language rather than human-like moral reasoning.

Investigating Implicit Bias In Large Language Models

Question

How do model type and social category interact to predict implicit bias in large language models?

Data

Replication/extension analysis using Bai et al. (2024) dataset measuring implicit bias in large language models through a prompt-based adaptation of the Implicit Association Test (IAT) based on human psychology. Analyzed a subset of 2,400 observations (300 responses per model) from 8 models across 4 domains (gender, health, race, religion) and 21 stereotype categories. Outcome: iat_bias (−1 to 1).

Method

Fit linear regression models in R to estimate one main effects of model and category and two model × category interaction effects. Tested whether prompt variation affected bias estimates and evaluated heteroscedasticity, reporting heteroscedasticity-robust standard errors.

Result

Found that implicit bias is highly context-dependent: bias levels vary across specific model–category combinations rather than being uniform across models or domains. The interaction model explained substantially more variance than model-only baselines, and prompt variation did not significantly change the bias patterns beyond the model–category interaction.
